Billie Jean King: The Pioneer¶

Overview¶

  • Full Name: Billie Jean Moffitt King (née Moffitt)
  • Born: November 22, 1943, Long Beach, California, USA
  • Turned Pro: 1968 (start of Open Era)
  • Retired: 1983 (singles), 1990 (doubles)
  • Career Prize Money: $1,966,487
  • Height: 5'4" (163 cm)
  • Plays: Right-handed (one-handed backhand)
  • Career Titles: 129 singles titles
  • Grand Slam Singles Titles: 12 (Australian Open: 1968; French Open: 1972; Wimbledon: 1966, 1967, 1968, 1972, 1973, 1975; US Open: 1967, 1971, 1972, 1974)
  • Grand Slam Doubles Titles: 16
  • Grand Slam Mixed Doubles Titles: 11
  • Total Grand Slam Titles: 39 (all-time leader)
  • Career High Ranking: World No. 1 (1966-1968, 1971-1972, 1974)
  • Years in Top 10: 17 (1960-1977)
  • Olympic Sport: Tennis was not Olympic sport during her prime years (returned in 1988)
  • Founder: Women's Tennis Association (WTA) and Women's Sports Foundation

Playing Style & Strengths¶

Net Game & Volleying¶

  • Aggressive Net Approach: Came to net behind serve and groundshots
  • Volley Technique: Crisp, punchy volleys with excellent placement
  • Half-Volley: Skilled at handling difficult half-volleys
  • Overhead Smash: Reliable and powerful overhead
  • Drop Volley: Effective touch shots at net

Serve¶

  • Kick Serve: Effective kick serve that bounced high to backhand
  • Placement: Excellent ability to hit spots and vary serves
  • Second Serve: Dependable second serve with good spin
  • Serve & Volley: Primary strategy on both first and second serves

Groundstrokes¶

  • Forehand: Flat, aggressive stroke hit early and hard
  • Backhand: Classical one-handed backhand, solid and reliable
  • Approach Shots: Penetrating approach shots that set up volleys
  • Passing Shots: Effective down-the-line and cross-court passing ability
  • Consistency: Remarkable ability to keep ball in play when needed

Movement & Court Coverage¶

  • Quickness: Exceptional first-step quickness despite smaller stature
  • Anticipation: Advanced court sense for predicting shots
  • Net Coverage: Excellent lateral movement at net
  • Recovery: Quick recovery after approach shots
  • Competitive Fire: Visible intensity that fueled performance

Mental & Competitive Traits¶

  • Fighter Mentality: Famous for never giving up on a point
  • Pressure Performer: Elevated level in biggest moments
  • Strategic Thinker: Excellent at analyzing opponents and adjusting tactics
  • Leader: Natural leader who inspired teammates and peers
  • Advocate Mentality: Fought not just for points, but for principles

Career Highlights¶

Grand Slam Singles Breakdown¶

  • Australian Open: 1 title (1968) - First Australian Open of Open Era
  • French Open: 1 title (1972)
  • Wimbledon: 6 titles (1966, 1967, 1968, 1972, 1973, 1975)
  • US Open: 4 titles (1967, 1971, 1972, 1974)

Career Slam Context¶

  • Era: Played during transition from amateur to Open Era
  • Surface Adaptation: Won on all four surfaces (grass, clay, hard)
  • Note: Australian Open was often skipped by international players in her era
  • Career Slam: Completed career Grand Slam with 1972 French Open victory

Other Notable Achievements¶

  • Year-End No. 1: 1966, 1967, 1968, 1971, 1972, 1974 (6 times)
  • Wimbledon Dominance: 6 singles titles, 10 ladies' doubles titles, 4 mixed doubles titles
  • US Open Success: 4 singles titles, 4 women's doubles titles, 4 mixed doubles titles
  • French Open: 1 singles title, 1 women's doubles title, 1 mixed doubles title
  • Australian Open: 1 singles title, 1 women's doubles title
  • Total Singles Titles: 129 (all-time leader)
  • Career Match Record: Approximately 695-155 (.817 winning percentage)
  • Team Sports: Instrumental in founding World TeamTennis (1974)

Pioneer Achievements & Advocacy¶

Women's Tennis Association (WTA)¶

  • Founded: 1973
  • Role: Founder and first president
  • Impact: Created unified women's professional tour
  • Legacy: Still governs women's professional tennis today

"Battle of the Sexes"¶

  • Event: September 20, 1973 vs. Bobby Riggs
  • Format: Best-of-five sets match
  • Result: Won in straight sets (6-4, 6-3, 6-3)
  • Prize Money: $100,000 winner-takes-all
  • Cultural Impact: Watched by estimated 90 million viewers worldwide
  • Significance: Pivotal moment for gender equality in sports

Equal Prize Money Advocacy¶

  • US Open 1973: Threatened boycott unless women received equal prize money
  • Result: US Open became first Grand Slam to offer equal prize money
  • Legacy: All four Grand Slams now offer equal prize money (completed 2007)

Virginia Slims Circuit¶

  • Role: Key player in founding the Virginia Slims Circuit (1970)
  • Original 9: One of the "Original 9" who boycotted USLTA over unequal pay
  • Impact: Launched first professional women's tennis tour

Women's Sports Foundation¶

  • Founded: 1974
  • Mission: To advance the lives of girls and women through sports
  • Impact: Continues to advocate for gender equity in sports

Rivalries¶

Margaret Court (22-10)¶

  • Grand Slam Finals: 6 (Australian Open '68; Wimbledon '66, '70; US Open '67, '69, '70) - King won 3
  • Era: Dominant rivalry of late 1960s
  • Style Contrast: Court's powerful all-court game vs. King's net aggression
  • Note: Court's overall record aided by Australian Open dominance (often skipped by internationals)

Rosie Casals (22-13)¶

  • Grand Slam Finals: 4 (French Open '69; Wimbledon '69, '70, '71) - King won 3
  • Doubles Partnership: Won numerous doubles titles together
  • Era: Represented changing of guard in late 1960s/early 1970s

Ann Jones (13-8)¶

  • Grand Slam Finals: 3 (Wimbledon '67, '69; US Open '68) - King won 2
  • Style Contrast: Jones' finesse and variety vs. King's power and aggression
  • Era: Transitional period in women's tennis

Chris Evert (8-6)¶

  • Grand Slam Finals: 2 (French Open '74, '75; Wimbledon '74, '75; US Open '74, '75) - Split 2-2
  • Evolution: Early Evert struggles vs. later developed consistency
  • Surface Split: Evert leads on clay (4-1), King leads on grass (3-2), relatively even on hard (2-2)
  • Era: Represented passing of torch to next generation

Legacy & Impact¶

On the Game¶

  • Gender Equality Pioneer: Most influential figure in fight for equal pay and opportunity
  • Professionalization Architect: Helped create structure for women's professional tennis
  • Advocacy Model: Showed athletes could use platform for social change
  • LGBTQ+ Visibility: Early high-profile athlete to come out as lesbian (1981)
  • Inspiration: Role model for generations of female athletes

Playing Style Influence¶

  • Serve-and-Volley: Demonstrated effectiveness of aggressive net game
  • Court Anticipation: Highlighted importance of reading opponent's intentions
  • Competitive Intensity: Set standard for fighting spirit and never-give-up attitude
  • Strategic Thinking: Showed value of analyzing opponents and adjusting tactics
  • Athleticism: Proved smaller players could dominate through quickness and strategy

Off-Court¶

  • Presidential Medal of Freedom: Awarded in 2009 by President Barack Obama
  • National Tennis Center: USTA Billie Jean King National Tennis Center (home of US Open)
  • Sports Illustrated: Named one of "Top 100 Athletes of the 20th Century"
  • Life Magazine: Named one of "100 Most Important Americans of the 20th Century"
  • BBC Sports Personality: Lifetime Achievement Award (2018)
  • Continued Advocacy: Remains active in LGBTQ+ rights and social justice causes

Famous Matches & Moments¶

1966 Wimbledon Final vs. Maria Bueno¶

  • Won in straight sets (6-3, 3-6, 6-1)
  • First Wimbledon title at age 22
  • Began her Wimbledon dominance (6 titles in 9 years)

1967 Wimbledon Final vs. Ann Jones¶

  • Won in 3 sets (6-3, 6-4, 6-3)
  • Successfully defended her Wimbledon title
  • Showed ability to handle crafty opponents

1967 US Open Final vs. Ann Jones¶

  • Won in straight sets (11-9, 6-4)
  • First US Open title
  • Began her US Open success (4 titles in 8 years)

1968 Australian Open Final vs. Margaret Court¶

  • Won in straight sets (6-1, 6-2)
  • First Australian Open title (first Australian Open of Open Era)
  • Completed career Grand Slam set across three surfaces

1972 French Open Final vs. Evonne Goolagong¶

  • Won in 3 sets (6-3, 6-3, 6-3)
  • Only French Open singles title
  • Completed her career Grand Slam

1972 US Open Final vs. Kerry Melville Reid¶

  • Won in straight sets (6-3, 7-5, 6-1)
  • Third US Open title
  • Demonstrated continued dominance at age 28

1973 Wimbledon Final vs. Chris Evert¶

  • Won in 3 sets (6-0, 7-5, 6-1)
  • Dominant victory over rising star
  • Showed ability to adapt to new generation's baseline game

1973 "Battle of the Sexes" vs. Bobby Riggs¶

  • Won in straight sets (6-4, 6-3, 6-3)
  • Watched by estimated 90 million viewers worldwide
  • Pivotal moment for gender equality in sports and society
  • $100,000 winner-takes-all prize (largest in tennis history at time)

1974 Wimbledon Final vs. Evonne Goolagong¶

  • Won in 3 sets (6-0, 6-4, 6-0)
  • "Double bagel" victory (6-0, 6-0 would have been golden set)
  • Sixth and final Wimbledon title
  • Demonstrated sustained excellence into early 30s

1975 Wimbledon Final vs. Evonne Goolagong¶

  • Won in 3 sets (6-2, 6-2, 6-3)
  • Sixth Wimbledon title at age 31
  • Showed longevity and adaptability

1983 Wimbledon Doubles Final (Last Match)¶

  • Partnered with Martina Navratilova
  • Lost in 3 sets (6-7, 7-6, 6-3)
  • Emotional farewell to Wimbledon Centre Court
  • Marked end of her singles career (though she played doubles until 1990)

Fun Facts & Trivia¶

  • First woman athlete to earn over $100,000 in a single year (1971)
  • Instrumental in getting Title IX passed (1972) prohibiting sex discrimination in education
  • Her jersey number 12 was retired by the World TeamTennis Philadelphia Freedoms
  • Named one of the "100 Most Important Americans of the 20th Century" by Life Magazine
  • Awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2009 (highest civilian honor in US)
  • The USTA National Tennis Center was renamed in her honor in 2006
  • First female athlete to have a major sports venue named after her
  • Known for her signature glasses that she wore on and off court
  • Married to Larry King (no relation to Larry King the broadcaster) from 1965-1987
  • Came out as lesbian in 1981, becoming one of first prominent athletes to do so
  • Continues to be active in LGBTQ+ advocacy and social justice causes
  • Her autobiography "Billie Jean King: An Autobiography" was published in 1982
  • Has been involved in numerous television broadcasts as commentator and analyst
  • Received the BBC Sports Personality of the Year Lifetime Achievement Award in 2018
  • Inducted into the Hall of Fame of Fame in 1987
  • Her foundation (Women's Sports Foundation) has helped millions of girls access sports
  • Known for her famous quote: "Pressure is a privilege"
  • Still actively involved in tennis as advocate, commentator, and mentor
  • Her life story was portrayed in the 2017 film "Battle of the Sexes" starring Emma Stone

Training Regimen Insights (Era-Appropriate)¶

  • 1960s-1970s Training: Less scientific than modern approaches, but innovative for time
  • On-Court Focus: Extensive serving and volleying drills, point play situations
  • Footwork: Ladder drills, shadow tennis, and movement patterns specific to serve-and-volley
  • Strength Training: Calisthenics, light weights, and core work (limited equipment available)
  • Endurance: Running, swimming, and on-court conditioning drills
  • Flexibility: Stretching routines, yoga was less common but she incorporated elements
  • Mental Preparation: Visualization, positive self-talk, and routine development
  • Nutrition: Basic understanding of fueling for performance (ahead of time for era)
  • Injury Prevention: Focus on proper warm-up, cool-down, and listening to body
  • Practice Partners: Varied practice with different styles to develop adaptability
  • Match Simulation: Practice sets and tiebreaks to develop competitive toughness
